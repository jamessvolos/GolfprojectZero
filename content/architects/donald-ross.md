Donald Ross learned the game in Dornoch, apprenticed under Old Tom Morris at
St Andrews, and emigrated to New England in 1899 with a set of clubs and a
greenkeeper's eye. Over the next four decades he became the most prolific
architect America has known, with a hand in something approaching four
hundred courses.

Ross's signature is subtlety at the green. His putting surfaces — the famous
turtleback greens of Pinehurst No. 2 the clearest example — repel the
imprecise approach and reward the shot played to the correct quadrant. The
punishment is rarely water or sand; it is the missed angle, the ball trickling
off a crown into a collection area, the tricky third from a spot you should
have avoided. Strategy, in Ross's hands, is a conversation held almost
entirely in the last forty yards.
