Seth Raynor was a Princeton-trained surveyor with no golf background when
Macdonald hired him to stake out the National. He turned out to be the ideal
partner: where Macdonald had the ideas, Raynor had the engineering discipline
to reproduce them with uncanny precision.

Raynor built the templates into a repeatable vocabulary — Redan, Biarritz,
Short, Eden, Cape, Punchbowl — and stamped them across dozens of American
courses: Fishers Island, Shoreacres, Chicago Golf Club, Yeamans Hall. His
greens are geometric, boldly contoured, and unmistakable; you can often name
a Raynor course from a single green complex. He died young, at 51, with a
full book of work unfinished. His engineered clarity is the counterweight to
MacKenzie's naturalism — the same strategic ideas, rendered in straight lines
and squared shoulders.
