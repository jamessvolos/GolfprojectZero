Before there was a profession called golf architecture, there was Old Tom
Morris — greenkeeper, clubmaker, four-time Open champion, and the man St
Andrews trusted with its ground. His work predates the Golden Age, but the
instinct that defines it runs straight through him: let the land dictate the
hole, and give the golfer a route rather than a sentence.

Morris widened the Old Course, separated its shared greens into the doubles
we know, and laid out or revised dozens of links from Prestwick to Muirfield
to Cruden Bay. He rarely moved earth. He read what was already there — the
folds, the burns, the whins — and set holes that asked a question of the
player without ever demanding a single perfect answer. Everything Macdonald
would later systematize into "templates," Morris had already met in the wild.
