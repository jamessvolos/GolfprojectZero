Tom Doak studied at Cornell, won a fellowship that let him spend a year
walking the great courses of Britain, and caddied at St Andrews to learn the
Old Course inch by inch. He came back with a conviction that has defined the
minimalist revival: the best architecture is the least architecture — find the
holes already latent in the land and disturb the ground as little as possible
to reveal them.

Doak's *Confidential Guide* made him famous (and feared) as a critic before
his own courses made him celebrated as a builder. Pacific Dunes, Ballyneal,
Barnbougle Dunes, and the restoration work at courses like Yeamans Hall put
the Golden Age strategic ideals back into practice with modern earthmoving
restraint. His holes are wide, firm, and full of options — closer to Old Tom
Morris than to anything built in the 1960s. He is, in temperament and thesis,
this site's contemporary standard-bearer.
