Alister MacKenzie was a Yorkshire surgeon who found in golf architecture a
better outlet for his gifts than medicine. A camouflage expert in the Boer and
First World Wars, he brought a painter's understanding of how the eye is
deceived to the shaping of ground — his bunkers and greens look as though the
land grew them.

MacKenzie wrote the closest thing the field has to a manifesto: thirteen
principles, chief among them that a great course must give pleasure to the
greatest number, that hazards should be visible and interesting rather than
merely penal, and that the ideal hole offers **an infinite variety of shots**
depending on the player's nerve and skill. Cypress Point, Augusta National
(with Bobby Jones), and Royal Melbourne are his monuments. He is the patron
saint of strategic design: the man who insisted, in print, that the golfer
should be offered a choice, never handed a verdict.
