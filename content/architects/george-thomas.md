George C. Thomas Jr. was a Philadelphia aristocrat, a decorated aviator, a
serious rosarian, and — almost as a hobby — the architect of some of
California's finest courses. He never took a fee; golf design was, for Thomas,
an amateur pursuit in the oldest and best sense.

His book, *Golf Architecture in America* (1927), is one of the sharpest ever
written on strategy. Thomas argued that "the strategy of the golf course is
the soul of the game" — that the whole point of design is to present options,
to reward the player who takes the more dangerous line with an easier next
shot. Riviera's par-3 6th, with a bunker set inside the green, is his famous
provocation. Bel-Air and Los Angeles Country Club complete a small, near-
perfect portfolio built almost entirely around the idea of the meaningful
choice.
