Bill Coore and Ben Crenshaw formed their partnership in 1986 and became the
most quietly influential design firm of the revival. Coore, who apprenticed
under Pete Dye and steeped himself in the writings of the Golden Age, is the
routing obsessive. Crenshaw — two-time Masters champion and a genuine scholar
of the game's history — brings the player's feel and the historian's reverence.

Their method is patient to the point of stubbornness: they walk a site
repeatedly, often for years, before committing a single line, and they build
with small crews who shape by hand. Sand Hills in Nebraska, laid across raw
prairie dunes with almost no earth moved, is their manifesto — a course that
feels discovered rather than designed. Their restorations of MacKenzie, Ross,
and Raynor work have done as much as anyone's to return American golf to
strategic first principles.
