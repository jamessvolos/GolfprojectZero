Pete Dye broke the postwar mold. When American design had drifted toward
long, green, aerial, and dull, Dye and his wife Alice traveled to Scotland in
1963 and came home converted — to railroad ties, pot bunkers, small greens,
and the deliberate discomfort of the eye. He reintroduced fear as a design
tool, and he was unashamed about it.

His holes are heroic to the point of theatrical: the island green at TPC
Sawgrass, the terrifying carries at the Ocean Course at Kiawah, the visual
trickery of Whistling Straits. Dye manipulated perception ruthlessly — a
bunker that looks in play but isn't, a green that looks smaller than it is.
Purists argue about whether it is strategy or intimidation. It is often both.
What is not in doubt is that Dye dragged the strategic conversation back to
the center of American architecture, and made the ground interesting again.
