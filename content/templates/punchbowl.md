The Punchbowl green sits cradled in a hollow, its surrounding slopes feeding
errant shots back toward the middle. It is the most forgiving of the classic
templates, and deliberately so — Macdonald and Raynor included it in their
vocabulary as a change of pace, a hole that gives rather than takes.

Its strategy is quieter than a Redan's or a Road's, but it is still a decision.
The approach is frequently blind or semi-blind over a fronting rise, so the
player must commit to a line they cannot fully see and trust the bowl to do the
rest. Position within the bowl still matters — the gathering slopes can leave a
long, curling putt if you finish on the wrong side. The Punchbowl earns its
place in the classification as proof that strategic design is not only about
hazard and fear; sometimes the interesting choice is how to accept a gift.
