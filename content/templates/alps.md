The 17th at Prestwick is the ancestral Alps: a long approach played blind over
a towering dune to a green concealed behind it, with the deep Sahara bunker
stretched across the front like a moat. You aim at a marker on the ridge, swing
hard, and wait to crest the hill and learn your fate.

Macdonald loved the Alps and rebuilt it at the National (the 3rd), preserving
the essential drama: a big, committing carry to a target you cannot see,
defended by a hazard you must clear completely. Modern architecture, obsessed
with fairness and sightlines, has largely abandoned the blind shot — which is
exactly why the Alps matters as a template. It insists that uncertainty, honestly
presented, is a legitimate and thrilling kind of strategy.
