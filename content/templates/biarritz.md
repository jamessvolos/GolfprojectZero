  The Biarritz asks for **a long, precise shot judged to a green split by a
  deep swale.** It is a long one-shotter (often 200+ yards) to a large, narrow
  green bisected by a dramatic gully or swale running across its middle, with
  bunkers hugging both sides. When the pin sits on the back plateau — the
  classic position — the player must either fly the ball all the way to the
  back (a huge, exacting carry) or land it short and use the swale to feed the
  ball up and through. The decision is one of raw commitment at distance: take
  on the full carry, or trust the ground and the swale to carry the running
  shot home. Precision, not power, but a great deal of both.

The Biarritz is the template that outlived its origin — the source hole at
Biarritz Golf Club in France has been altered out of existence, but the idea
Macdonald carried home from it survives everywhere. It is a long par 3 to a
big, narrow green with a deep swale slashing across the middle, flanked by
bunkers, usually with the flag on the far plateau.

The signature version keeps the swale as short grass, so the ground game is in
play: a running shot can be landed short and chased through the trough onto the
back tier. That is the strategic heart of it — the choice between an all-carry
aerial shot and a lower, running one that uses the swale. Yale, Fishers Island,
and the National all have celebrated Biarritz holes. It is Macdonald's proof
that a hole is fundamentally an idea, portable and survivable long after its
birthplace is gone.
