decisionBrief: |
  The most famous risk-reward decision in tournament golf, and — pointedly —
  not a template hole. A reachable par 5 doglegging left around Rae's Creek,
  which crosses directly in front of a green benched on the far bank. After a
  drive around the corner you stand in the fairway with a hanging lie and one
  question: go for the green in two over the creek, or lay up short and wedge
  it close for your birdie?
options:
  - label: Go for it in two
    order: 1
    reward: |
      Carry the creek and hold the green and you have an eagle putt and, at
      worst, a two-putt birdie — a two-shot swing on the field in a single
      brave stroke. On the back nine on Sunday, this is the shot that wins
      green jackets, and everyone watching knows it.
    risk: |
      The lie is rarely flat, the ball is often above your feet, and Rae's
      Creek sits exactly on the line, tight against the front of a green that
      falls back toward the water. Come up a fraction short and you are wet,
      dropping, and playing your fourth from a spot where bogey or worse is
      suddenly live. The same shot that wins the tournament can lose it.
    expectedNote: |
      Qualitatively a classic high-variance play: it widens the outcomes at
      both ends, adding eagles and 3s but also 6s and 7s. Its expected value
      swings enormously with the lie, the wind, and — crucially — the state of
      the tournament. The genius of the hole is that the *situation* keeps
      changing the right answer, which is why the same player attacks on
      Sunday and lays up on Thursday.
  - label: Lay up and wedge it close
    order: 2
    reward: |
      Lay back to a comfortable full-wedge distance, avoid the creek entirely,
      and play for a birdie the orthodox way. From 80–100 yards a tour player
      expects to hit it close, so you keep the birdie firmly in play while
      taking the disaster out of it — a low-variance route to a good score.
    risk: |
      You surrender the eagle and the two-shot swing, and you must still
      execute: the lay-up itself must avoid the creek and finish at a full
      number, and the wedge must hold a green that repels anything long or
      spun back toward the water. "Laying up" here is not a free par — it is a
      disciplined birdie attempt with its own demands.
    expectedNote: |
      Lower variance and, for most players on most days, a very healthy
      expected score. The lay-up concedes the top of the distribution to
      protect the bottom of it. The 13th is the definitive study in how the
      leaderboard rewrites the math: when you are ahead, the lay-up's tighter
      spread is worth more than the eagle; when you are chasing, only the
      carry gives you the swing you need.

The 13th is on this site precisely *because* it is not a template. It is proof
that the decision-problem thesis is bigger than Macdonald's classification —
that any great strategic hole, template or not, can be modeled as a fork with
honest prices on each branch. MacKenzie and Bobby Jones built a hole with no
right answer, only an answer that depends on your lie, the wind, and the number
on the board.

That is the deepest version of the site's argument. The 13th does not merely
offer a choice; it makes the *correct* choice change from shot to shot and day
to day. Strategy here is not a fixed solution to be memorized but a live
calculation to be made under pressure — which is exactly what makes it the most
watched second shot in golf.
